In the context below, \\(p\\) will denote prime numebers always.

As shown by Zhihu user Pandora Eartha that
\\[
\sum_{p\le n} \frac{1}{p}\sim \ln \ln n.
\\]
Assume the prime number distribution function \\(f(x)\\), then
\\[
\int^x dx' \frac{f(x')}{x}\sim \sum_{p\le x} \frac{1}{p} \sim \ln \ln x.
\\]
Differentiating on both sides yields
\\[
\frac{f(x)}{x} \sim \frac{1}{x\ln x}
\\]
and in turn suggests that \\(f(x)\sim 1/\ln x\\). Therefore
\\[
\pi(x) \sim \int^n f(x)dx \sim \int \frac{1}{\ln n}dx = \mathrm{Li}(n)
\\]
which is what we want.


The lemma we used can be found at https://www.zhihu.com/question/1918807288170386258/answer/1945782129632974139. Nevertheless, I will post a quick version of their proof.
\\[
\begin{aligned}
\ln\ln n \sim \ln \sum_{x\le n}\frac{1}{n} \sim \ln\prod_{p\le x}\sum_{i=1}^\infty \frac{1}{p^i}\\
=\ln \prod_{p\le x} \frac{1}{1-1/p}\\
\sim  \sum_{p\le x}\ln (1+1/p)\\
\sim \sum_{p\le x} \frac{1}{p}.
\end{aligned}
\\]
This could give a more precise estimate, but is beyond the scope of this short post.
